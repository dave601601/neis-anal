GIS & food culture · NEIS school-lunch data · 2021–2025

Does school lunch follow student food trends?

Yes — but only what the FORM · INSTITUTION · SPACE filter lets through.

What ends up on the lunch tray is decided by form, institution and space — not by taste, popularity, or “the big city.”
Three things everyone assumes turn out to be wrong.

What we did

2,355 high schools · ~2.2M lunches · 2021–2025 · each menu counted as appearances per 1,000 meals (Jeonbuk left out — its data only start in 2024).

How do you spot a “trend food” hidden in 2 million menu lines?

We didn’t hand-pick keywords. Instead a FastText word-embedding model learns which menu names mean similar things, and then grows each root word into every spelling it actually shows up as on real menus:

root: “mala”→ mala-tangmala-xiangguo mala-porkmala-soup …

We follow 7 trend roots (mala, tanghulu, Dubai chocolate, yakgwa, Greek yogurt, dujjonku, butter rice cake) and 8 health roots (vegan, vegetarian, plant-based, soy meat, soy/grain bulgogi, tofu cutlet/tender) the same way — so we catch every variation, not just the obvious names.

Three things everyone assumes — and why each is wrong

Finding 1 · FORM

Being popular isn’t enough — can it become a dish?

×6.9how much mala grew, 2021→2025

Mala spread fast because it slides straight onto a tray — mala pork, mala soup, mala stir-fry. Tanghulu and Dubai chocolate were just as viral with students, but they’re snacks: there’s simply no way to serve a candied-fruit skewer as a school lunch, so they barely appear.

“Popular → on the tray” is wrong. What matters is whether it can be cooked as a dish.

Finding 2 · INSTITUTION

The menu is a tug-of-war, not just “what students want”

2.9×trend food vs health food, 2025

Vegan and plant-based dishes aren’t there because students asked — adults push them from the top. When students don’t like them, they vote with their trays, and food waste piles up. The menu is a contest between what students crave and what the system values.

“Students drive the menu” is wrong — in both directions.

Finding 3 · SPACE  [GIS core]

Not the big city — it follows an eating-out map

I = 0.25Moran’s I for mala · p < 0.001

You’d expect Seoul to adopt trends first — it doesn’t. Mala clusters in Busan and Daegu, and a spatial test (Moran’s I) confirms this is real, not random. It’s a different map from Korea’s rural spicy-food regions: mala follows an urban dining-out geography, not local food culture.

“The big city leads trends” is wrong — uptake clusters in particular cities.

So why does the filter work this way?

① The institution filters form

School menus are planned a month ahead under strict nutrition rules — limited sugar and fat, no high-calorie snacks, and ingredients locked in by the 27th of each month. A candied snack can’t pass these rules; a mala dish can.

② Budget shapes the space

Some provinces add their own money for local, eco-friendly ingredients (Gyeongbuk adds 1,084 won a meal; Seoul adds nothing), giving those kitchens more room to experiment. But it’s only a loose pattern, not a cause — Busan adds 0 won yet is a mala hotspot.

③ Students push back

Vegan days started top-down (Gwangju, 2011). But students answered with food waste, and many regions quietly made them optional again. Where schools prepared students first — with education and cooking — approval bounced back (one Incheon pilot reached 81%).

The bottom line. School lunch does follow student trends — but only the ones the system can actually cook and serve, and only as unevenly as the map allows: mala ends up clustered in big cities, far from where Korea’s spicy food traditionally lives.

Honest limitations

The trend basket is ~80% mala, so really we’re tracking “mala adoption.” The budget link is only a qualitative pattern from 5 of 17 regions, not proof of cause. And NEIS data start in 2021, so there’s no “before the trend” baseline. Full statistics are in the paper.

Data & methods

NEIS lunch records (2,355 high schools, ~2.2M lunches, 2021–2025; Jeonbuk excluded). Keyword extraction with a FastText embedding. Spatial statistics — Moran’s I & LISA at the sigungu (district) scale — computed with PySAL. Budget figures from 5 of 17 education offices.
